Research Background

Light microscopy plays an important role in cell biology for studying living cells. However, conventional microscopy is limited by the diffraction properties of light, which significantly lower the imaging resolution and focus of sub-cellular structures. Achieving greater resolutions is particularly important in neurobiology, where observation of molecular processes in living neurons allows greater understanding of their morphology and plasticity. By understanding structure and function at this level, researchers can learn more about neurological and psychiatric diseases, such as Alzheimer's, autism spectrum disorders, or schizophrenia.

Scientists have turned to other imaging technologies to achieve higher resolutions, such as scanning electron microscopy, but these technologies can only observe non-living cells. To address this limitation, super-resolution microscopy (SRM) was developed to enable investigation of sub-cellular processes in living cells, beyond the diffraction barrier. While powerful, SRM can be conceptually complex and difficult to grasp.

To facilitate understanding, support, and adoption of the technology, I am creating a 3D animation to visually explain the higher-level principles of several SRM techniques, using visualization of neuronal dendritic spines and subcellular processes to deliver concepts. Additionally, I'm also developing a simple interactive training module to instill deeper understanding on one SRM technique introduced in the animation, called Switching LAser Mode (SLAM) microscopy, a novel technique developed by Dr. De Koninck's research team.
